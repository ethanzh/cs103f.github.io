Blog Post 8

People who do not want to pay for things are obviously the people who benefit the most from software piracy. They are able to acquire any software they want, regardless of price. This is beneficial only to the ‘consumer’ in this case, because no money is being paid into the system that helped produce this content that the ‘consumer’ is enjoying.

People justify piracy usually in one of two main ways. The first being that they do not see it as “stealing” in any sense because the owner does not lose their copy and they were never going to spend money on it in the first place. The second is comes from a more altruistic point of view. This group sees piracy as a means of liberation for underprivileged people who could not otherwise purchase the software. An example they might use to show that piracy can be justified is a person who is searching for a job and cannot afford Microsoft Word.

As discussed in the provided source, companies and independent developers can go bankrupt due to piracy. If none of the software users are willing to pay for the software, developers are unable to be compensated for their work. Pirated software also does not provide insight to the developers on the the popularity of the software. If the software is popularly pirated and rarely purchased, it may be hard to track actual total downloads of the software.

Technology has led to both the increase and decrease of software piracy, as there are some technologies which aim to prevent piracy and some which end up contributing to its rise. For instance, free-to-play games which allow users to access most of the game content without paying can motivate more people to stop pirating games. Digital Rights Management (DRM) systems, as explained in Patwegar’s article, also protect copyrighted content by only allowing users to access the content using encryption keys. However, opponents of DRM believe that it restricts users to specific devices, restricts creators to specific platforms, and can easily be bypassed. From a duty-based perspective, anti-piracy tech does have good intentions to prevent piracy, but in the cases of technologies like DRM, these technologies often do not completely prevent piracy as intended. Streaming services initially were thought to have decreased piracy by making content available legally. But now that there are many streaming services with exclusive content, there are too many services for consumers to subscribe to if they want access to all this content, so consumers have become more motivated to simply pirate the content. Advertisements on piracy sites also generate significant revenue for these piracy sites which contributes to increasing software piracy.
